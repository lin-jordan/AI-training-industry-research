# Content Style Guide

AI Training Atlas should sound clear, analytical, educational, neutral, and specific. Write for an AI-literate reader who does not yet understand the industry structure.

## Core writing rules

- Lead with what a concept or company does, then explain why it matters.
- Prefer concrete nouns and verbs over promotional adjectives.
- Separate factual summary, interpretation, and uncertainty.
- Use short paragraphs, descriptive headings, and parallel list structure.
- Define an acronym on first use unless the entry itself is the acronym.
- Use sentence case for titles and labels.
- Avoid rhetorical hype, vague superlatives, and claims of inevitability.

## Educational modules

- Start with a learning objective or orienting summary.
- Move from general system behavior to specific methods and examples.
- Explain inputs, process, outputs, and relationship to adjacent lifecycle stages.
- Acknowledge where a lifecycle diagram simplifies reality.
- Use examples that demonstrate the mechanism without implying universal behavior.

Preferred: “Post-training shapes a base model using demonstrations, preferences, tasks, and feedback.”  
Avoid: “Post-training unlocks the true power of AI and transforms raw models into intelligent agents.”

## Company profiles

- Describe observable products, capabilities, customer types, and lifecycle roles.
- Attribute marketing claims and reported figures.
- Do not infer company quality from funding, customers, investors, or growth claims.
- Distinguish official name, display name, and aliases.
- Use “the company states” sparingly; evidence labels and citations can carry attribution.

Preferred: “Fleet describes simulated worlds and real-world challenges for training and evaluating agents.”  
Avoid: “Fleet is revolutionizing agent training with world-class, cutting-edge environments.”

Preferred: “Headquarters: Not publicly disclosed.”  
Avoid: “Headquarters: N/A” or a location inferred from job listings.

## Glossary entries

Use four parts consistently:

1. **Short definition:** one sentence, understandable without opening another entry.
2. **Full definition:** mechanism, boundaries, and related terms.
3. **Why it matters:** its role in model development.
4. **Example:** a specific but non-misleading scenario.

Avoid circular definitions and treating one implementation as the only possible approach.

Preferred: “A benchmark is a standardized set of tasks and scoring rules used to compare model performance.”  
Avoid: “A benchmark benchmarks how good a model is.”

## News summaries

- State what happened, who reported or announced it, and the relevant date.
- Keep the summary factual and the takeaway analytical.
- Avoid urgency language such as “game-changing,” “massive,” or “shocking.”
- Do not convert an announcement into independent verification.

Preferred summary: “AfterQuery published work connecting expert-curated trajectories with results on an agent terminal benchmark.”  
Avoid summary: “AfterQuery achieved a stunning breakthrough that proves its revolutionary approach.”

Preferred takeaway: “Training-data providers increasingly combine data production with applied evaluation research.”  
Avoid takeaway: “AfterQuery will dominate the future of AI data.”

## Chart titles and captions

- Titles should state the measure and population, not a conclusion the chart cannot support.
- Captions should define the source, time period, unit, method, and limitations.
- Qualitative diagrams must state that visual distance and size are not quantitative.
- Never use “market share,” “growth,” “scale,” or ranking language without comparable evidence.

Preferred title: “Researched company profiles by primary subsector”  
Avoid title: “The leading companies in AI training”

Preferred caption: “Counts reflect profiles in this repository as of 2026-07-13; they do not measure market size.”  
Avoid caption: “The chart shows who controls the market.”

## Labels and metadata

- Use compact, literal labels: `Last researched`, `Source type`, `Primary capability`.
- Dates use `YYYY-MM-DD` in research records and metadata.
- Use approved subsector, status, category, and evidence-label values exactly.
- Do not rely on color alone to communicate status.

Preferred: `Company stated`  
Avoid: `Verified` when only a company source exists.

## Source markers

- Place source markers immediately after the supported claim when claim-level precision matters.
- Link to the most precise available source.
- Keep markers visually unobtrusive but keyboard accessible and descriptively labeled.
- A sources section should include title, publisher, review date, and evidence label.

## Empty and unknown states

Empty states should say what is absent, why, and what the reader can do next. Unknown fields should not appear as zeroes.

Preferred: “Not publicly disclosed.”  
Preferred: “No matching terms. Try a broader lifecycle concept.”  
Preferred: “External ingestion is not active; curated stories remain available.”

Avoid: “N/A.”  
Avoid: “No data” when the actual state is “not yet researched.”  
Avoid: empty charts or blank cards that imply a loading failure.

## Marketing-language substitutions

| Avoid | Prefer |
|---|---|
| leading | describe the relevant capability or attribute the claim |
| revolutionary | new, changed, or introduced, with specifics |
| best-in-class | reported performance on a named measure |
| powers the future | provides data, evaluations, or environments for… |
| trusted by | lists as customers or states it works with… |
| massive growth | reported growth over a defined period and unit |
| unique | differentiates through… or company-stated differentiator |

## Final editorial check

- Is every sentence necessary and specific?
- Can the reader distinguish evidence from interpretation?
- Are unknowns and company claims labeled honestly?
- Does terminology match the schema and glossary?
- Are links descriptive and sources precise?
- Does the content teach rather than promote?
